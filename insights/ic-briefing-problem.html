The IC briefing problem nobody names.

Book a Coffee

Senior partners do not adopt AI tools through demos. They adopt when something solves a problem they have this week. The firms that understand this get traction. The ones that don't run a lot of workshops that go nowhere.

Convolving Editors August 2025 5 min read Strategy
02The top-down mandate problem

"We're an AI firm now" is not an adoption strategy.

The announcement usually comes from a managing partner or a managing director, and it is made in good faith. The firm has decided that AI matters. A budget has been allocated. A working group has been convened, or a vendor has been selected, or both. The message to the team: we are taking this seriously.

What follows is a familiar sequence. A session is held. There are demos. There are breakout groups. There is a slide deck with "use cases" on it. There is an action item to "explore further." People leave the room having spent two hours of their attention on something they did not ask for and are not sure is relevant to their immediate work. Within a week, the session exists only as a calendar entry and a vague memory of a chatbot demo that could summarise documents.

This is not cynicism. It is the structural reality of how senior investment professionals allocate attention. They operate on weekly and monthly cadences tied to live deals, portfolio monitoring, and LP relationships. A generic demonstration of AI capability does not intersect with any of those cadences. It occupies a separate mental category: "interesting, probably important, not urgent." That category gets revisited quarterly, at best.

The mandate approach works when there is visible senior adoption alongside it. When the managing partner is not announcing that the firm is doing AI, but is instead using an AI-prepared briefing in the IC meeting this Thursday – that is different. But that requires having solved the senior adoption problem before the mandate, not through it.

03What actually works

One pain point. One working solution. One visible result.

The pattern that produces durable adoption at the IC level is not a programme. It is a sequence. One VP or principal with a specific, recurring pain point. A working solution that saves them forty-five minutes on something they do every week. One visible result that a partner notices and asks about.

That is how it propagates. Not through demonstration, but through adjacent observation. The partner sees that the principal's IC prep looks different – more structured, more consistent, produced faster. They ask what changed. The principal explains. The partner asks if it can be applied to one of their workflows. Now you have two people. Within three months you have four. Within six you have the shape of an adoption programme – but built from real use, not from announcement.

The pain-point specificity is not optional. "This saves time on research" does not propagate. "This turns four hours of portfolio monitoring prep into forty minutes, and the output is structured the way I would have structured it anyway" propagates – because it is concrete enough that someone else can immediately translate it to their own work. Vague benefits stay vague. Specific benefits have obvious analogues.

The forty-five minutes number matters too. Returns below thirty minutes per week tend not to create the kind of visible workflow change that others notice. Returns above ninety minutes start to feel implausible until demonstrated. The forty-to-sixty-minute range is the sweet spot for first-wave adoption: meaningful enough to change behaviour, credible enough not to require a proof-of-concept pitch.

04Why senior people are different

The question is never "can they use the interface." It is "does this save a decision."

Junior team members adopt tools through interfaces. Show them a capable tool, give them fifteen minutes to explore it, and the best ones will find uses you did not anticipate. Their adoption bottleneck is access and permission, not motivation.

Senior partners adopt tools through outputs. They do not have time to learn interfaces. They have time to receive outputs, evaluate them against their existing standard, and either incorporate them into their workflow or discard them. The question for IC-level adoption is never "can they learn to use this." It is "does this produce something they would otherwise have to produce themselves, or commission from someone else, or simply go without."

This distinction changes what you build. An AI tool designed for analyst adoption can afford to require setup, configuration, and a learning period – analysts have the time and the curiosity. An AI tool designed for partner adoption needs to produce output that lands in the existing workflow with near-zero friction. The partner should not be able to tell the difference between "I asked the system for an IC briefing on this company" and "I asked my principal for an IC briefing on this company" – except that the first one arrived in twelve minutes instead of four hours.

The IC briefing is a good test case precisely because it is genuinely high value. Partners read them. They make decisions based on them. They notice when they are good and when they are not. A well-designed AI briefing workflow is not a novelty – it is infrastructure that a partner will come to depend on, quietly and quickly, in the same way they came to depend on whatever process produced good briefings before.

A firm that runs an AI workshop for analysts, then waits for it to filter up, is usually waiting a long time. The partner's calendar does not have a slot for "see what the analysts built."
05The concurrency principle

Analyst builds and IC briefings have to move at the same time.

The most common structural mistake in firm-wide AI adoption is sequencing. The instinct is to start with the analysts – build something that works at the junior level, demonstrate value, then expand upward. It sounds sensible. In practice, it almost never reaches the partners.

The problem is the gap between what analysts build and what partners see. An analyst who has built a working research synthesis tool will use it quietly and efficiently, to their own benefit. They will not go to their managing director and report that they have saved six hours per week and here is a presentation. They will do the work faster, produce better output, and say nothing. The managing director will not notice that the analyst is working differently. Nothing propagates.

The programmes that actually achieve IC-level adoption run both tracks concurrently. While the analysts are building real tools – a prompt library, a workflow prototype, a document analysis process – a separate and simultaneous track is running directly for IC-level participants. Not demos. Not a workshop on AI possibility. A direct engagement with one or two specific problems that a partner or MD has this month, producing working output within the first two or three sessions.

The analyst builds something real. The IC sees a real result. When those two things happen in the same two-week window, something changes. The IC asks questions. The analyst has answers. The conversation that was never going to happen bottom-up happens naturally, because there is now a shared object to talk about – not a demo, but a piece of work that both parties have reason to care about.

This is harder to design than a sequential programme. It requires the engagement to run two workstreams simultaneously, which means two sets of relationships, two sets of goals, and two cadences that have to stay loosely synchronised without becoming one meeting. But the alternative – sequential adoption that stalls between the analyst layer and the IC layer – is not really an alternative. It is a way to run a programme that leaves no lasting infrastructure and produces no senior champions. Firms that have been through one of those know the feeling: a lot of effort, some useful tools at the junior level, and a partner who missed the whole thing.
