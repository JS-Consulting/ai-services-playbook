01Field note

Prompts are policy.

When you write a shared prompt for your team, you are not writing a shortcut. You are encoding your firm's analytical standards into language. That is worth doing carefully.

Convolving Editors September 2025 5 min read Method
02The vague prompt problem

Vague prompts produce inconsistent output because they are vague policy.

"Summarise this CIM." It is probably the most common prompt in use at European PE and VC firms right now. It is also a policy statement that has been written carelessly, and it shows in the output.

The model, faced with a two-hundred-page document and no constraints, makes decisions. It decides what matters and what doesn't. It decides how long the summary should be. It decides whether to lead with the market opportunity or the management team or the financials. It decides whether to flag risks or present the document charitably. It decides whether to use headers or prose, bullet points or paragraphs.

These are not neutral formatting choices. Every one of them is an analytical decision that should have been made by the analyst – or, better, encoded once by the partner into a prompt that the whole team uses. When the decision is left to the model, you get output that is confident and fluent and inconsistently useful. One analyst's CIM summary looks nothing like another's, except that both were "generated by AI."

The vague prompt is vague policy. It outsources the analytical framework to a language model that has no knowledge of how your firm thinks, what your IC cares about, or what the partners actually read. The output is only as good as the implicit assumptions the model makes on your behalf – and those assumptions are neither documented nor reproducible.

03What a good prompt specifies

Three things every shared prompt needs to define.

Context: who is reading this and why

A CIM summary for a first-pass screening decision is different from one prepared for an IC memo. A research brief for a principal doing competitive diligence is different from one prepared for an LP update. The model needs to know who the output is for, what decision it is supporting, and what level of detail is appropriate. Without this, it will default to something generic and moderately useful – the kind of output that gets skimmed and disregarded.

Specifying context does not require a paragraph of setup. It requires one sentence: "This summary will be read by a deal partner making a binary go/no-go screening decision. Prioritise reasons to pass." That single constraint changes the output more than any formatting instruction.

Constraints: what to skip, what to flag, what format

The absence of constraints is where summaries bloat into uselessness. A model given no constraints will produce balanced, comprehensive output that covers everything and prioritises nothing. That is not a summary. That is a rearrangement.

Good constraints are specific. Skip the company history section entirely. Cap the output at four hundred words. Flag any revenue concentration above thirty percent as a risk item regardless of how the company presents it. Always include a section on the management team, even if the document downplays it. Always note if audited financials are absent. These are the rules a senior analyst carries in their head after two years at the firm. A prompt library makes those rules explicit and applies them consistently.

The failure mode: what wrong looks like

This is the piece almost nobody includes, and it is probably the most important. Every shared prompt should describe what a bad output looks like, so the reader knows what to check. "If the model presents a single-customer business without flagging concentration risk, the summary is wrong regardless of what else it says." "If the revenue figures in the summary do not match the table on page 47 of the document, do not trust the numbers." "If the output is longer than one page, it has not been filtered – send it back."

The failure-mode note does two things. It makes the reviewer's job faster, because they know exactly what to check rather than reading the whole thing with vague suspicion. And it teaches the team, over time, where the model's weaknesses cluster – which changes how you write the next iteration of the prompt.

04Prompt libraries as institutional IP

Six months of refinement is a real asset. Treat it accordingly.

A firm that has spent six months iterating on its memo-synthesis prompt has built something real. Not a competitive moat in the conventional sense – the underlying model is available to everyone. But something more durable: an encoded version of how the partners think, what the IC structure rewards, and where the firm has learned to be sceptical.

That encoding takes time to build. It requires the partners to articulate things they have never written down before – their actual analytical framework, not the version they would describe in a recruiting pitch. It requires the analysts to document where the model fails, version after version, until the failure modes are narrow and well-understood. It requires someone to notice that the prompt written in January produces different output than it did in January, because the model was updated and nobody tracked it.

Firms that treat prompt libraries as throwaway infrastructure – a shared Google Doc that grows and is never pruned – lose this asset quickly. The library becomes inconsistent, the prompts accumulate contradictory instructions, and the output quality regresses without any obvious cause. The library needs ownership, versioning, and periodic review. It is, in effect, a piece of software. Firms that have not yet thought of it that way will eventually be forced to.

The firms we work with that have done this well share one characteristic: one person owns the library. Not a committee. One person with the authority to add, revise, and deprecate. That person is almost always a principal or VP-level operator, not a junior analyst – because the decisions encoded in the prompts are analytical decisions, not formatting decisions.

The question is not "does the AI summarise well." The question is "does it summarise the way we think." Those are different, and only the second one requires you to write down how you actually think.
05The maintenance problem

Prompts decay. Someone needs to own the library.

A prompt written for GPT-4 in early 2023 produces different output on Claude 3.7 in 2025. Not slightly different – meaningfully different, in ways that matter for analytical work. The model's defaults for length, hedging, structure, and confidence have changed. Instructions that were necessary to counteract one model's tendencies are unnecessary or counterproductive for another. Instructions that were not needed have become critical.

This is not a hypothetical. It is happening at firms that deployed shared prompts eighteen months ago and have not touched them since. The output has drifted, slowly enough that nobody noticed until a partner read two consecutive summaries side by side and asked why one was three pages and the other was four bullet points.

Prompt maintenance is not glamorous work. It involves periodic evaluation runs – feeding the same set of test documents through current prompts, comparing output to the target standard, identifying where the gap has widened. It involves reading the model provider's release notes seriously, not as a consumer looking for new features, but as an operator looking for behaviour changes that will affect production output. It involves the discipline to deprecate a prompt that no longer works, even if it took three months to build.

The firms that have built durable prompt libraries have done so by treating this as a recurring operational task, not a one-time build. Quarterly review cycles, documented test sets, version history, and an owner who takes the quality regression personally. That is the standard. Firms that built a library, felt good about it, and moved on are now running on prompts that have silently degraded – and they will not know until the output fails in a situation where it matters.
