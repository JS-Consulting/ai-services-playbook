Your first AI workflow.

Book a Coffee

The first workflow you build sets the template for everything that follows. Get it right and you have a pattern. Get it wrong and you have a cautionary tale that will take six months to overcome.

Convolving Editors February 2025 5 min read Method
02The common mistake

Starting with the impressive use case.

The pattern is consistent across firms of every size. Someone returns from a conference, or finishes a demo, or reads a piece about what AI is doing for a competitor. Enthusiasm is high. The mandate is clear: we are doing this. The first question asked is: what is the most transformative thing we could build?

This is the wrong question, and it reliably produces the wrong answer.

The transformative use case – the one that would genuinely change how the firm underwrites, or allocates, or monitors – requires three data sources you haven't connected yet, produces output that partners will scrutinise with a level of scepticism they reserve for first-year analysts presenting at IC, and has a definition of success that the team will argue about for six weeks before anyone builds anything. This workflow takes four months to build, two months to argue about, and fails to impress when it is finally shown because the expectations have outrun what any model can deliver against messy, incomplete, real-world data.

Momentum dies. Not because AI is not useful – it is – but because the first attempt was sized for a victory lap, not a first step. The cautionary tale that follows follows the firm for longer than the failed project. "We tried that last year" is one of the most durable obstacles in organisational change.

03What to build first

Boring, frequent, forgiving.

The right first workflow has four properties. It takes 30 minutes or more today. It happens at least weekly. It has a clear, evaluable output format – meaning someone can read the result and say, within sixty seconds, whether it is good enough. And it is reviewed by a human before anything consequential happens downstream.

These criteria are not exciting. They are deliberately not exciting. The goal of the first workflow is not to demonstrate what AI can do at its best. The goal is to demonstrate that AI can be reliably useful – every week, without drama, for an output the team actually uses.

Memo drafting almost always works. A model given a CIM and a structured prompt will produce a first-pass memo that a junior analyst would not be embarrassed to have written. It needs editing. It misses nuance. It occasionally gets a number wrong in a way that anyone reviewing it will catch immediately. But the 45-minute task becomes a 15-minute review, and that is a genuine change in how the team's time is spent.

Research synthesis works. Partner meeting prep works. Portfolio update summaries work. These tasks share the same profile: structured inputs, familiar output format, human review before the output goes anywhere consequential. They are also genuinely valuable – the time savings are real and recurring, and the people doing the work will notice the difference within a week.

The first workflow does not need to be ambitious. It needs to be the one that a sceptical partner uses once, notices saved them thirty minutes, and uses again the following week without being asked. That is the outcome you are building toward. Everything else – the connected systems, the automated pipelines, the workflows that run without anyone touching them – comes after that.

04What to skip

Later, not never.

The workflows to avoid for a first build are not permanently off limits. They are later. The distinction matters, because framing them as "later" keeps the roadmap honest – teams that say "never" to consequential AI workflows are eventually wrong, and teams that say "now" to everything are building trust deficits before they have built any trust.

Skip anything that requires judgment calls on consequential decisions. Loan approvals. Deal go/no-go recommendations. Regulatory correspondence. Not because models cannot contribute to these processes – they can – but because the trust that makes those contributions valuable has to be built on a foundation of reliable performance on lower-stakes tasks. A team that has watched the model perform well on twenty research summaries will receive its input on deal screening differently than a team that met the model for the first time on a screening decision.

Skip anything that touches external parties directly. The failure mode for an AI system that emails an LP, responds to a regulator, or sends a term sheet amendment is not a wasted hour of editing time. It is an external incident. These workflows have their place. That place is well after you understand how the model behaves under the full range of inputs your firm generates.

Skip anything where the failure mode is "we sent the wrong thing to an LP." More broadly: if the first question you have to answer when the model makes an error is "who do we call," the workflow is not ready. The first workflows should have failure modes where the answer is "we fix the draft before it goes anywhere."

"The first workflow is not chosen for its ambition. It is chosen for its reliability. A workflow that works every time, for a task that happens every week, changes the culture faster than a dramatic demo that works once."
05The pattern that works

Four weeks. One workflow. Build from there.

Week one: pick the workflow, write the prompt, run it on five historical examples, evaluate manually. Not a meeting to discuss the prompt – a session where someone writes it, runs it, reads the output, and edits the prompt based on what is wrong. This should take half a day, not a week. If it is taking longer, the workflow was not scoped precisely enough.

Week two: run it live with one team member, track their time. Not the whole team – one person, with an explicit before and after measurement. Do not announce it as a pilot. Ask a specific analyst to use the workflow for their actual work this week and report back on whether it saved time and whether the output was usable. The feedback from one person using it on real tasks is worth more than twenty people trying it on test cases.

Week four: expand to the team, refine the prompt, document what bad output looks like. The documentation step is the one most teams skip and the one that matters most. "Bad output" is specific – a list of the ways the model has failed on this particular workflow, with examples. Without it, every new team member who encounters a bad output treats it as a surprise. With it, they recognise the pattern, apply the fix, and move on. The prompt library starts here.

Month three: the team uses it without thinking about it. They do not remember what it was like to do the task without AI assistance. When someone asks how long the workflow takes, they give the AI-assisted number without noting the caveat. That is the moment adoption has happened – not when the tool was deployed, not when the first successful output was produced, but when the new method has become the default and the old one requires conscious effort to recall.

The compounding works in both directions from there. A team that has one workflow embedded at month three has the organisational trust and the prompt-writing fluency to add a second workflow cleanly. A team that tried three workflows simultaneously and has none of them embedded at month three is starting over. The first workflow is not the end of the programme. It is the proof of concept for how the programme runs.
