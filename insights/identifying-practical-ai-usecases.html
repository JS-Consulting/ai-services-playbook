Finding the usecases worth building.

Book a Coffee

Most AI opportunity lists fail not because the ideas are bad. They fail because the triage is missing. Here is the triage.

Convolving Editors April 2026 8 min read Method
02Why lists fail

The ritual is familiar, and it is broken.

A firm commits to "doing AI." A workshop is scheduled. Two weeks later, a list of forty usecases exists in a spreadsheet, each with a tidy score for impact, feasibility, and alignment. Someone prints it. Someone circles four. Work begins.

Six months in, two of the four are quietly abandoned. One is a chatbot that nobody uses. One has turned into a dashboard.

This is not a talent problem. The people running these programmes are usually excellent. The problem is the list itself. Scorecards built by committee drift toward whatever the committee already wanted to build. Impact-and-feasibility matrices are almost infinitely redecoratable. And the vocabulary – "usecase" – flattens a dozen different kinds of work into a single unit, as if automating a monthly report and reimagining underwriting were the same shape of project.

The lists fail not because the ideas are bad. They fail because triage is missing.

The triage we run with clients uses three questions. They are boring on purpose. They are designed to be answered in minutes, not weeks, and to be defensible to a sceptical partner without a deck. If a proposed usecase cannot answer all three cleanly, it does not move forward – regardless of what its impact score says.

03The three questions

Three questions. All three, or it waits.

1. Does this job already have a clean outcome metric?

If the people doing the work cannot tell you what good looks like in a sentence, a model cannot either. "Faster reporting" is not a metric. "Memo turnaround from eight days to three" is. "Better sourcing" is not a metric. "Twenty qualified intros per partner per quarter, measured against conversion" is.

The test: can you describe success as a single observable number that changes? If yes, you have something a model can be pointed at, evaluated against, and improved. If no, you have a conversation to finish before you have a usecase.

This is where most "AI strategy" lists collapse on inspection. A surprising number of high-scoring ideas dissolve when asked what good looks like – because the underlying process was never measured, and nobody wants to be the one who starts.

2. Is the failure mode boring?

Every AI system will fail. The only question is whether the failure matters.

A system that drafts a research memo summary can fail in small, forgivable ways – a missed nuance, a dated reference, a mischaracterised footnote. A human reads the draft, catches the error, moves on. The worst case is a minute of wasted attention.

A system that decides whether to flag a transaction, approve a loan, or reply directly to a regulator has an interesting failure mode. The worst case is a compliance incident, a reputational hit, or a lawsuit. Interesting failure modes are not forbidden – but they demand different architecture: human-in-the-loop, tighter evaluation, fallback paths, and usually a longer timeline.

The question is not "how accurate is the model." The question is "what happens when the model is wrong." If the answer is short and boring, build. If the answer requires a second meeting, build carefully – or build something else.

3. What is the marginal cost of the current answer?

Automation is expensive. Not to run – to build, evaluate, maintain, retrain, and trust. The cost is justified when the current answer is also expensive.

If a junior analyst already produces the output in thirty seconds, automating that task is theatre. The AI system will cost more to build and maintain than it saves. Worse, it will displace a piece of work that was quietly teaching that analyst the business – and get nothing back.

If the current answer takes thirty minutes of senior time, or thirty hours of junior time, or is simply never produced because the cost is prohibitive, the economics invert. Now automation is not replacing a cheap habit; it is creating new capacity.

The three failure modes of this question are the most common of all: building AI for tasks that were cheap to begin with, building AI for tasks that turn out not to exist (people insist the process is manual; the data shows it already runs on a macro), and building AI for tasks that are rare enough that the engineering cost never amortises.

The usecases worth building are the ones where the business already knows what good looks like, the failure mode is boring, and the current answer is expensive. Everything else is a conversation – not a project.
04A worked example

120 ideas. Four bets. Eight working days.

A mid-cap European insurer came to us with 120 proposed usecases from an internal innovation process. The list ran across claims, underwriting, customer operations, finance, and – inexplicably – marketing.

We ran the three questions against each item. The exercise took eight working days for two people.

Seventy-one items failed question one. They had no outcome metric. Phrases like "improve customer experience," "accelerate reporting," and "enhance decisioning" appeared more than thirty times. Every one of those items became a short follow-up note to the sponsor: before this is a project, agree on the number.

Thirty-three items failed question two. Claims adjudication, regulatory correspondence, fraud escalation, and a surprising share of "AI-assisted" underwriting proposals. They were not killed – several were re-scoped with explicit human-in-the-loop architectures and moved to a separate track with its own timeline and governance.

Twelve items failed question three. Tasks that were already cheap (internal FAQ deflection, meeting summarisation for teams that held few meetings), and two tasks that, on inspection, were done exactly twice a year.

Four items survived. A claims-triage workflow with a well-established SLA metric and a forgiving failure mode. A sanctions-screening assist where the model flagged but a human decided. A memo-synthesis workflow for the deal team. And a customer-segmentation refresh that had previously taken a data scientist six weeks – because it ran once a year and nobody could justify the headcount.

Those four went into build. Three shipped on time. The fourth was killed in week six when the data turned out to be worse than advertised – and the team kept the kill as a result, not a failure.

05How to run it

A few operating principles.

Run it with a senior. One person, not a committee, with the authority to kill a usecase without a follow-up meeting. The triage collapses into decoration the moment it becomes collaborative.

Run it in two weeks. Not a quarter, not a phase. The value of the exercise is speed – a backlog of 100 items loses its fear the moment someone commits to a rolling decision cadence. If the process stretches, the list metastasises.

End with four numbers. How many items survived, how many were re-scoped into a carefully-governed track, how many were killed, and how many were sent back for a clearer outcome metric. The shape of those four numbers is its own diagnostic. If every item survives, the triage is too lenient. If nothing does, the pipeline was seeded wrong.

Share the kill list. The one artifact more valuable than the build list is the written reason each item was killed. It teaches the organisation how decisions get made, lets people propose new work with sharper framing, and – critically – closes the door on zombie projects that would otherwise resurface in the next planning cycle.

Most AI programmes do not fail for want of ideas. They fail because the triage that would have protected the good ones was never run.
