What's Actually Working

Two wins and one instructive failure

Abstract descriptions of "AI-powered workflows" are worthless. Here is what it actually looks like when a fund puts AI to work on a real problem. Two use cases that delivered, and one that did not.

Use Case A: Pre-deal screening

Screening 200+ targets down to 15 for IC review

The Situation

A mid-market fund needed to screen 200+ potential targets in specialty chemicals down to a shortlist of 15 for IC review.

What They Did

They used Claude to process public filings, broker reports, and three proprietary databases. The model cross-referenced acquisition criteria against 14 financial and strategic filters, flagged 22 candidates, and drafted one-page profiles for each.

What Changed

What took an associate two weeks was done in four hours. Three of the final 15 were targets the team had not previously identified.

The Surprise

The model flagged a carve-out opportunity inside a larger conglomerate that the team had written off. It ended up being the lead target.

Use Case B: Portfolio operations

Monthly KPI extraction across 12 portfolio companies

The Situation

A fund needed to extract, normalize, and compare monthly KPIs across 12 portfolio companies, each reporting in a different format, to produce a board-ready summary with variance commentary.

What They Did

They built a pipeline that ingests management reports (PDFs, Excel, and email attachments), extracts 23 standard KPIs, normalizes them into a single template, and auto-generates a variance commentary draft for each portfolio company.

What Changed

The process went from five days of analyst work to a half-day review. The operating partner now spends their time on the commentary rather than the data collection.

The Surprise

The system caught a revenue recognition inconsistency in one portfolio company's reporting that had gone unnoticed for three quarters.

The Dead End

When AI-generated IC memos created more work, not less

What They Tried

A fund attempted to use AI to draft full IC memos from raw deal materials, with the goal of giving associates a "90% done" first draft.

Why It Failed

The model hallucinated financial figures in 3 out of 10 memos, inserted plausible but fabricated comparable transactions, and produced a writing style so generic that partners could immediately tell it was AI-generated. The team reverted to manual drafting within two weeks.

The Lesson:

Use AI to gather and structure inputs; keep the synthesis and narrative in human hands.

The Call

Our take on what happens next

Pre-deal AI has been the consensus play for three quarters. The funds getting ahead now are the ones betting on execution and portfolio operations, where the tools are worse but the edge is bigger.

The gap between "we're exploring AI" and "AI is in our IC process" just became the gap between top-quartile and median returns. Every mid-market fund we spoke to is using AI for some form of pre-deal research. That is table stakes. The differentiation has shifted to who can embed AI into the operational fabric of their portfolio companies and their own internal processes.

The next twelve months will separate funds into two camps: those treating AI as a productivity tool for existing workflows, and those redesigning workflows around what AI makes possible for the first time.

Three moves to make this quarter

  1. Run your last three IC memos through Claude with this prompt and compare. Ask the model to identify the three strongest counterarguments to each investment thesis. Compare its output to what your deal team produced. That is your baseline.
  2. Stop using AI to summarize documents you were going to read anyway. Start using it to find the three things in the document you would have missed. The value is not in compression. It is in coverage.
  3. Build a shared prompt library for your deal team. Document the 10 prompts your best AI users rely on. Distribute them. The difference between a team where one person uses AI well and a team where everyone does is a shared playbook.
One thing to stop doing

Stop buying AI point solutions for problems that Claude handles out of the box. You are paying $50K/year for a wrapper around a $20/month model.
